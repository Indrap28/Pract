History

Sepsis is a life-threatening medical condition that occurs when the body's response to an infection leads to a systemic inflammatory response.

It is a medical emergency that can progress rapidly and affect multiple organ systems. Sepsis is sometimes referred to as "septicemia" or "blood poisoning."

Early recognition and treatment of sepsis are critical for a better outcome. It's important to seek immediate medical attention if you suspect sepsis or if you or someone you know has symptoms of infection and signs of sepsis. Timely intervention can significantly improve the chances of survival and recovery.




About

Sepsis is a severe and life-threatening medical condition that arises when the body's response to infection results in systemic inflammation, which can lead to organ dysfunction and failure. It is considered a medical emergency and requires immediate medical attention. The signs and symptoms of sepsis can vary but often include the following:

  • Fever or hypothermia (very low body temperature).
  • Rapid heart rate (tachycardia)
  • Rapid breathing (tachypnea)
  • Altered mental state, confusion, or difficulty staying awake
  • Low blood pressure (hypotension)
  • Signs of organ dysfunction, such as difficulty breathing, low urine output, abnormal liver function, clotting abnormalities, and more

    • Treatment

    The primary goals of sepsis treatment are to stabilize the patient, manage the infection, and prevent or treat organ dysfunction.

  • Antibiotics: Administered promptly to target and treat the underlying infection.
  • Intravenous (IV) Fluids: To maintain blood pressure and provide hydration.
  • Vasopressors: Medications to raise blood pressure if it remains low despite fluids.
  • Supportive Care: Depending on the extent of organ dysfunction, additional interventions may be necessary, such as mechanical ventilation for respiratory failure.
